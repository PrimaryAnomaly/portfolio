## Context

Most multi-robot systems depend on centralized controllers or reliable wireless communication — assumptions that break down in communication-denied environments like planetary surfaces, disaster zones, or contested infrastructure. Fleet-Coord removes both assumptions. Two simulated [Unitree G1](https://www.unitree.com/g1/) humanoid robots learn to cooperate on manipulation tasks using only signals a human could understand and produce: verbal countdown cues and force feedback through the shared object.

The design constraint is deliberate: a human must be able to swap in for either robot mid-task without protocol changes. This forces the coordination interface to stay legible — no binary handshakes, no wireless pings, just observable behavior.

## Architecture

The system is built in four layers. The **environment layer** wraps MuJoCo physics at 1000 Hz and exposes a Gymnasium interface with 202-dimensional observations per robot (joint states, object pose, hand contact forces, partner speech tokens) and 51-dimensional actions (43 joint targets plus an 8-token discrete speech output). A speech channel simulates verbal communication with 20 ms delay and 200 ms persistence — realistic constraints that prevent instant coordination.

The **training layer** bridges the environment to ETH Zurich's RSL-RL framework for multi-agent PPO. Both robots in each environment share rewards, encouraging cooperative behavior. A curriculum manager tracks rolling-window success rates and automatically advances training stages (approach, grip, lift, carry) once performance exceeds threshold.

The **teacher-student architecture** uses a privileged teacher policy (MLP with layers [256, 256, 128]) that sees partner hidden state during training. After convergence, this distills to a student policy using only observable inputs — the path to real hardware deployment.

## Speech Channel

Rather than continuous audio, robots emit discrete tokens from an 8-word vocabulary: silence, "three", "two", "one", "lift", "down", "stop", "regrip". These are sampled directly from the policy's action distribution alongside motor commands. The channel introduces a 1-step transmission delay and 10-step persistence window, meaning the partner has roughly 200 ms to register each cue before it fades.

This design is debuggable (replay logs show "robot A said token 4 at step 125"), composable (token sequences build natural countdown patterns), and maps cleanly to real voice commands for human-robot teaming.

## Reward and Evaluation

Reward shaping is config-driven through YAML files. Stage 1 rewards include dense signals for distance decrease toward the object, stable hand contact, grip duration, and penalties for falling. Each component is logged independently to TensorBoard for debugging — when success metrics showed 100% but visual inspection revealed collapsed robots with spurious contacts, per-component breakdowns identified the gap.

The fix hardened evaluation to require box-specific geom contact checks and verify both robots remain upright (base tilt < 0.7 rad). Success metrics now enforce structural invariants, not just contact flags.

| Layer | Technology |
|---|---|
| Physics | MuJoCo 3.4.0 at 1000 Hz |
| Scene Composition | dm-control MJCF builder |
| RL Framework | RSL-RL v1.0.2 (PPO) |
| Neural Network | PyTorch 2.0+ with CUDA |
| Robot Model | Unitree G1 with Dex3-1 hands (43 DOF) |
| Observation Space | 202 dims per robot |
| Action Space | 51 dims (43 motor + 8 speech) |
| Policy Frequency | 50 Hz (20 sim steps per action) |

## Current Status

Stage 1 (approach and grip) is under active training with strict evaluation metrics. Initial GPU runs exposed false-positive success rates, leading to the metric hardening described above. Next steps include warm-starting from pretrained G1 locomotion checkpoints and running controlled A/B comparisons against training from scratch. The long-term goal: fleets of humanoid robots building habitats where communication infrastructure doesn't exist yet.
